Cherishing the slow way of doing something

I cherish the slow and sometimes difficult way of doing my art homework when I’m working on something personal, like the book I’m creating in my designing creative nonfiction course. Even though there is a deadline and a grade attached, I intentionally don’t rush it because I want the final object to feel meaningful rather than just complete. In this case, I am making the book as a gift for my mom for Mother’s Day, so the process matters as much as the outcome. I find myself embracing constraints like limited materials, time spent revising layouts, and the patience required for cutting, arranging, and rewriting pages. These limitations actually push me to be more thoughtful and intentional with every design choice. The slower process allows me to notice details I would normally overlook if I were trying to finish quickly for convenience. It also gives me space to connect emotionally with what I’m creating, rather than treating it like an assignment. From this, I learn that fulfillment often comes from effort and care, not efficiency alone. It suggests that technologies and tools are most valuable when they support attention and creativity rather than replace the time it takes to make something personal.
